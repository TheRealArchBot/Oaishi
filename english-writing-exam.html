📖 Full Essay — Artificial Intelligence
INTRO

Imagine a world where machines diagnose diseases faster than doctors, write software, and drive cars without human error. This world is no longer science fiction — it is unfolding right now through the rapid rise of artificial intelligence. AI, the simulation of human intelligence by machines, is reshaping industries, transforming daily life, and redefining what it means to work. Despite widespread concerns about job displacement and ethical risks, artificial intelligence is a transformative force that enhances productivity across industries, revolutionizes healthcare, and unlocks new possibilities in education.

BODY 1 — Productivity

First and foremost, artificial intelligence dramatically enhances productivity and efficiency across industries. Repetitive, time-consuming tasks such as data entry, customer service responses, and logistics planning are now automated by intelligent systems, freeing human workers to focus on creative and strategic functions. According to a McKinsey Global Institute report, AI technologies could add up to $13 trillion to global economic output by 2030. Rather than replacing the workforce entirely, AI acts as a powerful amplifier of human capability. Consequently, industries from manufacturing to finance are experiencing unprecedented levels of innovation and output.

BODY 2 — Healthcare

Furthermore, artificial intelligence is revolutionizing healthcare in ways that save millions of lives. AI-powered diagnostic tools can analyze medical images — X-rays, MRIs, CT scans — with greater accuracy and speed than human radiologists, enabling earlier detection of deadly diseases like cancer. IBM's Watson Health system has demonstrated the ability to identify cancer treatment options in minutes, a task that previously required weeks of specialist review. Drug discovery, once a decade-long process, is being accelerated by AI algorithms screening millions of molecular combinations. The integration of AI in medicine represents one of the most profound humanitarian achievements of our era.

BODY 3 — Counterargument + Refutation

Critics argue that artificial intelligence poses a serious threat to employment, and this concern deserves genuine consideration. As automation displaces manual and clerical jobs, millions of workers in transportation, retail, and manufacturing face an uncertain future. The World Economic Forum has warned that AI could eliminate 85 million jobs by 2025. However, history offers reassurance: every major technological revolution, from the Industrial Revolution to the rise of computing, ultimately created more jobs than it destroyed. The same WEF report projects AI will simultaneously create 97 million new roles in data science, AI maintenance, and digital strategy. The solution lies not in rejecting AI, but in investing in workforce reskilling. Dismissing AI out of fear would be as shortsighted as rejecting electricity.

CONCLUSION

In conclusion, artificial intelligence, though not without challenges, is one of the most transformative technologies humanity has ever developed, accelerating productivity, advancing healthcare, and reshaping education. The key lies in guiding it with ethical frameworks and inclusive policies. The future belongs not to machines alone, but to those wise enough to work alongside them.

📖 Full Essay — Men-Women Equality
INTRO

In 2023, a Bangladeshi woman was denied a promotion not because of her qualifications, but because her manager believed a male colleague was a more "suitable" leader. This scenario, repeated millions of times daily across the globe, reflects a deeply embedded problem: gender inequality. Despite remarkable progress, women continue to face systematic disadvantages in workplaces, homes, and institutions. Gender equality is not merely a feminist cause but a fundamental human right that demands the elimination of workplace discrimination, the reformation of restrictive social norms, and the guarantee of equal educational opportunities for all.

BODY 1 — Workplace Discrimination

To begin with, workplace discrimination remains one of the most persistent barriers to gender equality in our society. In Bangladesh, women are concentrated in low-wage sectors like garment manufacturing, while senior corporate leadership remains dominated by men. Globally, the gender pay gap persists, with women earning approximately 82 cents for every dollar earned by men for comparable work, according to UN Women. The so-called "glass ceiling" — an invisible barrier preventing women from rising to top positions — continues to limit millions. Enforcing equal pay laws and encouraging women's leadership are essential steps toward an equitable workforce.

BODY 2 — Social Norms

Moreover, restrictive social and cultural norms continue to limit women's freedoms and reinforce inequality in everyday life. In many parts of Bangladesh, deeply rooted traditions dictate that women are primarily responsible for household duties and childcare, regardless of their professional ambitions. Early marriage, affecting 59% of Bangladeshi girls according to UNICEF, cuts short girls' education and traps them in economic dependency. Notably, rigid gender roles harm men too, pressuring them to suppress emotions and bear sole financial responsibility. Reforming these norms through education and legal protections benefits everyone.

BODY 3 — Counter + Refutation

Some traditionalists contend that biological differences between men and women naturally justify distinct social roles. They argue that women's roles as mothers and caregivers are universal, rooted in biology rather than culture. This argument, however, confuses biology with destiny. Research consistently shows that differences in career outcomes between genders are shaped primarily by social conditioning, not innate ability. Countries like Norway and Iceland, which actively promote gender equality, demonstrate that when barriers are removed, women lead companies and governments as effectively as men. Equality does not erase biological difference; it simply ensures those differences do not determine one's rights.

CONCLUSION

In summary, gender equality remains an urgent and achievable goal requiring the dismantling of workplace discrimination, the challenging of harmful social norms, and the guarantee of equal opportunity for every individual regardless of gender. Societies that invest in gender equality are more stable, more prosperous, and more innovative. Equality is not a privilege for some — it is a right for all.

📖 Full Essay — Student Union Elections (Bangladesh)
INTRO

University life in Bangladesh is frequently marked by challenges students feel powerless to address: rising tuition fees, inadequate hostels, outdated curricula, and campus harassment. For decades, students have had no formal democratic channel to raise their voices. The revival of student union elections could change this fundamentally. Student union elections in Bangladeshi universities can effectively address the major problems students face by establishing democratic representation, advocating for meaningful academic reforms, and improving campus welfare and safety.

BODY 1 — Democratic Voice

First, student unions provide students with a legitimate democratic voice within university governance structures. Without elected representatives, Bangladeshi universities are governed entirely by administrations and political appointees who may be disconnected from the realities of student life. When students elect their own representatives through transparent elections, those representatives can formally negotiate with administrations and advocate for policies that directly impact students. Democratic representation means decisions about fee increases, academic calendars, and campus infrastructure are made with student input rather than imposed upon them.

BODY 2 — Academic Reform

Furthermore, active student unions can drive vital academic and administrative reforms that improve educational quality. Outdated syllabi, examination irregularities, favoritism in grading, and the absence of counseling services are issues students regularly identify but cannot formally address without a union. At Dhaka University, periods of active student union leadership historically coincided with greater administrative transparency and more responsive student services. Unions can also campaign for merit-based scholarship distribution and petition against session jams — issues that directly affect thousands of students every year.

BODY 3 — Counter + Refutation

Opponents of student union elections argue that such elections inevitably become politically corrupted, hijacked by party agendas rather than serving student interests. This concern is well-founded: historically, party-affiliated student wings have used campuses for political violence, extortion, and coercion, turning union elections into tools of control. However, this problem calls for structural reform, not abolition. Implementing independent electoral commissions, strict code-of-conduct regulations, transparent campaign finance rules, and prohibition on party-aligned candidacies can insulate student unions from political corruption. Countries like the UK and Australia maintain vibrant, independent student unions — Bangladesh can achieve the same with proper institutional safeguards.

CONCLUSION

In conclusion, student union elections, when conducted transparently and independently, are a powerful mechanism through which students gain democratic representation, push for academic reform, and build safer campus environments. The problems facing Bangladeshi university students are not inevitable — they are the product of a system that lacks accountability to those it serves. The student voice must not be silenced; it must be organized, empowered, and heard.

📖 Full Essay — Traditional Examinations Are Discriminatory
INTRO

Every year, thousands of Bangladeshi students spend weeks memorizing pages of notes, only to forget everything the day after their exam. The system they are trapped in rewards recall over reasoning, punishes anxiety over ability, and defines intelligence through a single standardized test. Traditional examinations have been the dominant form of academic assessment for centuries — yet their dominance is increasingly questioned. Traditional examination systems are fundamentally discriminatory rather than encouraging because they privilege rote memorization over critical thinking, systematically disadvantage students with diverse learning needs, and fail to assess the full range of human intelligence.

BODY 1 — Memorization Not Understanding

To begin with, traditional examinations primarily reward memorization rather than genuine understanding or intellectual development. Students who can reproduce textbook content verbatim within a fixed time frame are rewarded with high grades, while those who think critically or approach problems creatively often struggle to perform within such rigid formats. Renowned education theorist Sir Ken Robinson argued that conventional schooling and its examination culture systematically suppress creativity — one of the most valuable human capacities. In Bangladesh, "note-guessing" — predicting which topics will appear on exams — encourages selective study rather than comprehensive learning. Education should ignite curiosity, not extinguish it.

BODY 2 — Disadvantages Diverse Students

Moreover, traditional examinations systematically disadvantage students from less privileged backgrounds and those with diverse learning needs. In Bangladesh, students with access to expensive coaching centers and private tutors consistently outperform their rural counterparts, not because they are more intelligent, but because they have greater access to exam preparation resources. Students with dyslexia, anxiety disorders, or attention difficulties are further penalized by formats demanding rapid, silent, individual writing — a format that suits only one type of learner. When a single format determines academic success, the system measures privilege, not potential. That is discrimination dressed as objectivity.

BODY 3 — Counter + Refutation

Defenders of traditional examinations argue that standardized testing provides an objective, fair method of evaluation that prevents favoritism. They contend that uniform exams allow performance to be compared across institutions and regions, creating a meritocratic system where students are judged on knowledge alone. This argument conflates uniformity with fairness. A system is only fair if all participants begin with equal opportunities — in Bangladesh, where educational quality varies enormously between urban and rural schools, the examination format measures starting advantage as much as academic merit. Alternative models — portfolios, project-based evaluations, oral presentations, continuous assessment — can maintain rigor while offering more inclusive, multidimensional measures of achievement.

CONCLUSION

Ultimately, traditional examinations, by privileging memorization, disadvantaging diverse learners, and ignoring the multidimensional nature of intelligence, function as instruments of discrimination disguised as tools of assessment. This is not an argument against evaluation itself, but against the narrow high-stakes format through which it is conducted. Bangladesh's universities must move toward a more equitable, holistic model — one that encourages every student rather than filtering out all but the best memorizers.

📝 1 · Cause-Effect — Corruption
Structure: causes (why it happens) → effects (what it causes). Use transitions like because, as a result, consequently.

Corruption is a deeply rooted crisis whose causes and devastating effects form a destructive cycle that undermines national progress. Political corruption arises primarily from weak governance, an absence of accountability, and a culture of impunity where powerful individuals face no consequences for unethical actions. Low wages for public officials further drive corrupt practices, as underpaid bureaucrats resort to bribery to supplement their incomes. As a result of rampant corruption, public funds meant for schools, hospitals, and infrastructure are siphoned away, leading to deteriorating services and widening inequality. Consequently, investors lose confidence in the country's institutions, causing reduced foreign investment and slower economic growth. Furthermore, corruption erodes public trust in government and the judiciary, weakening the very foundations of democracy. Until root causes are systematically addressed through stronger institutions and a culture of transparency, corruption's effects will continue to devastate society.

📝 2 · Comparison-Contrast — University Life vs Professional Life
Use point-by-point: compare them on each aspect one at a time. Don't describe university entirely first then professional — mix them per point.

University life and professional life represent two distinct phases of adulthood, each with its own rhythms, responsibilities, and rewards. In university, students enjoy a relatively flexible schedule, choosing lectures and organizing time around assignments and social activities; in contrast, professional life demands strict punctuality, fixed working hours, and consistent performance with little room for casualness. While university fosters intellectual exploration — encouraging students to question ideas and pursue knowledge for its own sake — the professional world is primarily results-oriented, valuing efficiency and output above curiosity. Similarly, both environments require teamwork and communication skills, yet the stakes differ significantly: a failed group project may cost a grade, while a failed workplace effort may cost clients and reputations. Furthermore, university provides a financial safety net in the form of family support, whereas professional life demands full financial self-sufficiency. Despite these differences, both university and professional life serve as essential training grounds, preparing individuals to navigate challenges, build relationships, and grow as capable members of society.

📝 3 · Process — Writing an Essay
Steps must be in ORDER. Every step needs a transition (first, next, then, finally). Don't skip steps.

Writing a successful essay requires following a systematic process that transforms rough ideas into a polished, coherent piece of writing. The first step is prewriting, during which the writer generates ideas through techniques such as brainstorming, freewriting, or clustering, ultimately arriving at a focused thesis and rough outline. Next, the writer moves to drafting — composing a first version without worrying about perfection, the goal being simply to get ideas on paper. After completing the first draft, the writer engages in revising, the most critical step, which involves reconsidering the essay's argument, structure, and clarity: reorganizing paragraphs, strengthening the thesis, ensuring each topic sentence clearly supports the central argument. Then comes editing, a more focused process addressing sentence-level issues including grammar, vocabulary, and word choice. Finally, the writer proofreads carefully, catching any remaining errors before submitting the final draft. Following this process consistently results in essays that are not only grammatically correct but logically compelling and clearly articulated.

📝 4 · Division-Classification — Examinations
Divide your topic into CLEAR, distinct categories. Each category should not overlap with others. Use "can be classified as," "the first type," etc.

Examinations can be classified into several distinct categories based on their format and purpose, each designed to assess student knowledge in a different way. The first and most common category is the written examination, which itself divides into essay-type tests requiring students to construct extended written arguments; short-answer tests demanding brief but precise responses; and multiple-choice tests presenting options from which students select correct answers. A second category is the practical examination, used primarily in science, engineering, and medical programs, where students demonstrate technical skills in a laboratory or clinical setting rather than through written responses. The third type is the oral examination, in which students respond verbally to questions posed by examiners — a format testing communication skills and the ability to think under pressure. Finally, standardized or public examinations, such as the SSC and HSC in Bangladesh, represent a fourth category: large-scale, nationally administered tests designed to compare performance across institutions. Understanding these categories helps students and educators choose the most appropriate assessment form for any given learning objective.

📝 5 · Descriptive — A Casual Day at University
Use your 5 senses: what you SEE, HEAR, SMELL, FEEL. Don't narrate a story — just describe the scene like you're painting it.

On any given afternoon, the campus of a Bangladeshi public university is a vivid, unhurried canvas of student life. The wide, tree-lined pathways are dotted with students moving in small clusters — some clutching notebooks, others scrolling through phones, all unhurried in the golden afternoon light. The air smells faintly of earth and fried snacks drifting from nearby tea stalls, where vendors ladle out hot cha into thick-walled glasses. Around the central field, groups of students sprawl on the grass, some deep in conversation, others simply staring at the sky. The corridors of the faculty buildings hum with a low murmur of voices — the occasional burst of laughter, the squeak of chalk on a classroom board, the distant echo of footsteps on concrete stairs. Near the library steps, a lone student reads with unusual concentration, oblivious to the scattered pigeons pecking at crumbs beside him. It is neither dramatic nor extraordinary, but there is a particular, irreplaceable warmth to this scene — the unhurried ease of a place where people come not only to study, but simply to be young.

📝 6 · Narrative — A Casual Day at University
Tell a STORY in sequence. First person (I). Include action, reflection. The difference from descriptive: here you're DOING things, not just describing a static scene.

That Tuesday started the way most of my university days do — with a missed alarm and a rushed breakfast. I arrived at the faculty building just as the 9 AM class was beginning, slipping into a seat at the back and hoping my professor would not notice my disheveled state. The lecture on macroeconomics drifted over my head at first, but then something clicked — a real-world example about inflation that suddenly made the textbook make sense. By noon, my friend Karim and I had claimed our usual corner at the campus canteen, arguing passionately over whether the tea here was better than the one at the roadside shop near the gate. After lunch, we wandered to the library — mostly to sit and talk rather than study — and spent an hour debating nothing in particular. The afternoon slipped away in a haze of easy conversation, one short nap under a shady tree, and a shared plate of jhalmuri from the stall by the main gate. By evening, as the campus quieted and the sky turned pink, I realized that no extraordinary thing had happened that day — and yet, walking home, I felt oddly full, as though the day itself had been enough.

What is an Essay? 🗂️
Definition · Components · Diagram

Definition

An essay is a formal piece of academic writing that presents, develops, and supports a single central argument (called the thesis) through organized paragraphs. It always has three parts: an introduction, body paragraphs, and a conclusion.

Short Notes 📒
Answer any 3 of 5 — all 5 covered here
a) Common Method of Writing an Introduction — The Funnel Method
Think of a funnel: wide at the top (broad topic), narrow at the bottom (specific thesis). You start general, get more specific with every sentence, and end with the sharpest, most specific thing: your thesis.
BROAD → General statement about the world or topic
↓ narrowing
Background / context sentences
↓ narrowing
SPECIFIC → Thesis Statement

Other hook methods: Anecdote (short story), Rhetorical question ("What if...?"), Shocking statistic, Direct quotation.

b) Principles of Writing a Strong Thesis Statement
ASPPP: Arguable · Specific · Positional · Plan of development · Placed at the end of intro
  1. Must be arguable: It's a position someone can disagree with. Not a fact. ✗ "AI exists." ✓ "AI transforms healthcare, education, and industry."
  2. Must be specific: No vague language. ✗ "AI is important." ✓ "AI enhances productivity, revolutionizes healthcare, and reshapes education."
  3. Single unified idea: Don't cram unrelated claims together.
  4. Includes plan of development: Signals what the 3 body paragraphs will argue.
  5. States writer's position: The reader knows exactly where you stand.
  6. Placed at the end of the intro: Last sentence (or last two) of the intro paragraph.
c) Three Purposes of the Introduction
  1. Engage the reader / grab attention: The intro must hook the reader immediately, giving them a reason to keep reading.
  2. Provide background and context: It supplies what the reader needs to know about the topic before the argument begins.
  3. State the thesis and plan of development: It tells the reader what the essay argues and how it will be structured — a roadmap for what follows.
d) Three Purposes of the Conclusion
  1. Restate the thesis: Revisit the central argument in fresh language — remind the reader what has been proven, without copying the original thesis.
  2. Summarize the main supporting points: Briefly recap the key arguments from the body paragraphs, giving a sense of completeness.
  3. Leave a lasting impression: End with a final thought — a call to action, a broader implication, a prediction, or a memorable observation. This is what the reader carries away.
Never introduce a NEW argument in the conclusion. Never end abruptly. Always close the loop.
e) Basic Structure in the Diagram of a Paragraph
Topic Sentence The main idea of the paragraph. A mini-thesis — one clear claim that directly supports the essay's thesis. Every reader should know what this paragraph is about just from this sentence.
Supporting Detail 1 Evidence, example, fact, statistic, or expert opinion that proves the topic sentence.
Supporting Detail 2 Further evidence, often from a different angle, to reinforce the point.
Analysis Your explanation of HOW and WHY the evidence proves the topic sentence. This is YOUR voice. Never drop evidence without explaining it.
Concluding Sentence Wraps up the paragraph and may transition to the next paragraph's idea.
TS → SD → A → CS = Tell me → Show me → Analyze it → Close it. If you can say all four things, you have a complete paragraph.

Worked Example 1 — "The Dangers of Excessive Smartphone Use"

Read the essay below, then see all 8 sub-questions answered. This is exactly the format you'll face in the exam.

INTRO

In today's hyper-connected world, smartphones have become an inseparable part of daily life. While these devices offer undeniable conveniences, their excessive use poses serious threats to physical health, mental well-being, and social relationships. Three major problems that arise from smartphone overuse are deteriorating physical health, growing mental health issues, and weakening of real-world social bonds.

BODY 1

First of all, excessive smartphone use causes significant damage to physical health. People who spend hours hunched over their screens often suffer from "tech neck," a condition caused by straining the neck muscles. Eye strain and disrupted sleep cycles are also common, as the blue light emitted from screens suppresses melatonin production. According to health researchers, teenagers who use phones for more than four hours daily report more physical complaints than those who use them moderately.

BODY 2

Furthermore, smartphone addiction is closely linked to deteriorating mental health. Constant exposure to social media platforms creates unrealistic comparisons, leading to anxiety and low self-esteem. Notification alerts trigger dopamine cycles that mirror addictive behaviour. Studies have shown that heavy social media users are significantly more likely to report feelings of loneliness and depression than those who limit their use.

BODY 3

Finally, ironically, the device designed to connect people is increasingly isolating them in real life. Families sit together at dinner tables yet remain absorbed in their individual screens. Friendships are reduced to likes and emoji reactions rather than meaningful conversations. The art of face-to-face communication — reading body language, practising empathy — is slowly eroding among younger generations.

✅ Sub-Q 1 — Thesis statement + keywords

Thesis

"Three major problems that arise from smartphone overuse are deteriorating physical health, growing mental health issues, and weakening of real-world social bonds."

Keywords

smartphone overuse physical health mental health social bonds

These four terms echo through every paragraph — that's how you identify keywords. They are the nouns the entire essay revolves around.

✅ Sub-Q 2 — Plan of development

The thesis sentence itself is the plan of development here — it lists the three body points in order: (1) deteriorating physical health → (2) growing mental health issues → (3) weakening of social bonds. This roadmap tells the reader exactly what the three body paragraphs will argue.

Sometimes the plan of development is a separate sentence after the thesis. Here it is embedded inside the thesis — both forms are correct.
✅ Sub-Q 3 — Topic sentence of body paragraph 1

Topic sentence

"First of all, excessive smartphone use causes significant damage to physical health."

Notice: it echoes the thesis keyword "physical health" and introduces the paragraph's single argument. Always the first sentence of body paragraph 1.

Grammar Corrections 🔧
Fragments · Run-ons · Agreement · Modifiers · Punctuation

Error Types — Know What Each One Is

FragmentIncomplete sentence. Missing subject, verb, or both. A dependent clause standing alone.
Run-onTwo complete sentences smashed together without proper punctuation or conjunction.
S-V AgreementSubject and verb don't match (singular/plural).
Pronoun AgreementPronoun doesn't match the noun it refers to.
Dangling ModifierA describing phrase that doesn't attach to the right noun (the one doing the action).
Misplaced ModifierA describing phrase placed too far from the noun it describes, causing confusion.
a · Fragment / Illogical Clause Order
Michelle tried each of the appetizers on the table and then found that when she arrived, her appetite was gone.
When Michelle arrived, she tried each of the appetizers on the table but found that her appetite was gone.
She must ARRIVE before she can TRY the appetizers. The original sentence has her trying food before arriving, which makes no sense. Reorder: arrival → trying → finding appetite gone.
b · Fragment (Detached Phrase)
I love to cook and eat Italian food, especially, sterity and labhagna. I make everything from scratch.
I love to cook and eat Italian food, especially spaghetti and lasagna, which I make entirely from scratch.
"Especially, sterity and labhagna" is a fragment — it can't stand alone. Attach it to the main clause. Also "sterity and labhagna" = spaghetti and lasagna (misspelled in original). The two sentences should be combined.
c · Run-on Sentence
My grades are very good in this semester. My social life grades only a C.
My grades are very good this semester; however, my social life only gets a C.
Two contrasting thoughts must be properly connected. Use a semicolon + conjunctive adverb (however). Also: "grades only a C" needs the verb "gets" — "grades" cannot function as a verb meaning "receives a grade of."
d · Run-on Sentence
Self-cleaning public toilets are available in few American cities have installed them.
Self-cleaning public toilets are available, but few American cities have installed them.
Two complete sentences fused without any punctuation or conjunction. Fix: add a comma + coordinating conjunction (but), or make one a subordinate clause.
e · Subject-Verb Agreement
The serious look in that girl's eye worrying me.
The serious look in that girl's eye worries me.
Subject = "look" (singular). Verb must be "worries" (singular present tense). "Worrying" is a participle — it cannot be the main verb by itself. The phrase "in that girl's eye" is a prepositional phrase and does NOT affect the verb.
f · Pronoun Agreement
I lifted the pot of my hot potatoes, but the slipped out of my hand.
I lifted the pot of hot potatoes, but it slipped out of my hand.
"The" is a determiner — it CANNOT replace a noun. The pronoun needed here is "it" (referring to "the pot" — singular). You can't use "the" as a pronoun. Ever.
g · Dangling Modifier
Frustrated by piles of homework, Rafiq's temptation was to watch television.
Frustrated by piles of homework, Rafiq was tempted to watch television.
The phrase "Frustrated by piles of homework" must describe the subject immediately after the comma. The subject must be the PERSON who is frustrated — Rafiq. Instead, the original makes "Rafiq's temptation" the frustrated thing, which is illogical. Fix: make Rafiq the subject.
h · Misplaced Modifier
Rafiq returned the burger to the supermarket that was spoiled.
Rafiq returned the spoiled burger to the supermarket.
"That was spoiled" is placed next to "supermarket," making it sound like the supermarket is spoiled. The modifier must sit next to the noun it describes — "burger." Move "spoiled" directly before "burger."
i · Punctuation + Fragment
Printed out an article title too much homework? From the magazine Reader's Digest.
He printed out an article titled "Too Much Homework?" from the magazine Reader's Digest.
The original has no subject — it's a fragment. Add a subject (He/She). The article title needs quotation marks. The magazine name needs italics (or underline). "From the magazine..." is a dangling fragment — attach it to the main sentence.
j · Punctuation + Wrong Sentence Break
The top of the hill offered an awesome view of the military cemetery. Sounds of headstone were arranged in perfect rows.
The top of the hill offered an awesome view of the military cemetery, where the headstones were arranged in perfect rows.
"Sounds of headstone" = nonsensical. It should be "headstones" (plural). The second sentence describes the cemetery view — it belongs attached to the first sentence via a relative clause ("where..."), not as a separate sentence.

Applied Example B — Questioning (5W+1H): "Emotional State Affects Our Memory"

QuestionExploration
WHO is affected?Everyone — students during exams, trauma survivors, grieving people, people in love.
WHAT happens?Strong emotions enhance memory encoding (flashbulb memories); chronic stress hormones (cortisol) damage the hippocampus, impairing recall.
WHERE in the brain?Amygdala (emotion) and hippocampus (memory) are physically connected — they directly influence each other.
WHEN does emotion affect memory?At encoding (when experience happens) AND at recall (we remember mood-matching information more easily — "mood congruence").
WHY does this happen?Evolution: emotionally significant events are critical for survival, so the brain prioritizes storing them.
HOW strong is the effect?Extreme emotion → vivid but sometimes inaccurate memories. Mild positive mood → better learning. Severe anxiety → impaired recall.
Thesis from Questioning: Our emotional state profoundly shapes both the formation and retrieval of memories, as the brain's emotional and memory systems are deeply intertwined — causing strong emotions to enhance recall while chronic stress impairs it.
Q9
Assertion vs Opinion 🎯
What's the difference? How do you tell them apart?
Opinion = "I think chocolate is the best ice cream." Nobody can argue with you — it's just your feeling. Assertion = "Chocolate ice cream outsells all other flavors in Bangladesh." Now THAT can be proven, debated, supported. That's what academic writing needs.
PointOpinion ❌Assertion ✅
NaturePersonal belief or preferenceConfident claim supported by evidence
Based onFeelings, preferences, emotionsLogic, facts, evidence, reasoning
ObjectivitySubjective — varies person to personCan be argued and defended
Language"I think," "I feel," "I believe," "In my opinion"No hedging — states directly
In academic writingWeak. Avoid it.Required. Use it everywhere.

Side by Side Examples

Opinion ❌ — Weak

"I think corruption is really bad for our country."

"I believe AI will take everyone's jobs."

"In my opinion, exams are unfair."

"I feel like gender equality is important."

Assertion ✅ — Strong

"Corruption systematically diverts public funds, undermining infrastructure and public trust."

"While AI displaces certain jobs, it simultaneously creates new categories of employment."

"Traditional examinations disadvantage students from lower socioeconomic backgrounds."

"Gender equality produces more stable, prosperous, and innovative societies."

The Quick Test: Can you ask "How do you know? Can you prove that?" about the claim? If YES → assertion. If the only answer is "Because I feel that way" → opinion. In your essay, EVERY thesis and EVERY topic sentence must be an assertion.

Key Distinction for the Exam

An opinion is what the person feels. An assertion is what the writer claims to be true and is willing to defend with evidence.

The content can be the same — the difference is whether it is grounded in logic and evidence or purely personal feeling.

Important: An assertion doesn't have to be correct — it just has to be arguable. That's what makes it academic.
